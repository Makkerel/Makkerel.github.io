LK-99 And The Unknown Side of Science

March 18, 2025

Around midnight on Friday, July 21st, 2023 a paper was uploaded to arxiv.org an online archive of research papers. I was probably sleeping at the time and an event like this was nothing unusual. It was not published anywhere and the authors were not well known. No one should have cared about this preprint and yet the entire world would soon be driven into frenzy. Why? Because it claimed to have discovered the holy grail of material science, a room temperature superconductor called LK-99. In the simplest terms, the paper was claiming the new material would be in essence a replacement for copper. Superconductors are materials that perfectly conduct electricity unlike a material like copper which will lose a bit of the electricity as it passes through. Pretty much all electronics would become more efficient with a room temperature superconductor.

By Wednesday the following week, all my social media from Reddit to Twitter had someone talking about LK-99. One tweet had over 3 Million Views! Maybe it was the release of ChatGPT months earlier or just the desire for change due to the pandemic but it was this discovery that transfixed the public like few had before. Yet the claim of this superconductor was unproven and quickly started the rollercoaster that was the scientists from around the world scrambling to replicate the findings of the paper.

Superconducting magnet engineer chiming in.

This result could be very big news, and overnight revolutionize all of electronics and energy. It might not.

Major national and university laboratories rushed to be the first to confirm the findings. The Wikipedia page for LK-99 even had an entire section that was being updated to show the status of replication for different labs. This entire situation, to put it lightly, was odd as usually, labs would patiently wait on one another or until the paper was actually properly published but here it felt like a competition. The reason for this was the fact that based on the paper it felt that anyone and their grandma could seemingly reproduce LK-99. The process outlined was almost too simple and only needed copper phosphate and lead sulfate which many scientists already had lying around their lab. Some more chemically savvy public members even announced that they would attempt to DIY the material by themselves within their homes. Some of these DIYers would stream or tweet their progress online for all the world to witness.

Indeed the draw of LK-99 was that it was cheap and a room temperature superconductor. It was so simple to make that many scientists felt wrong to not at least attempt to replicate the findings. The public's desires for results were answered when on August 1st, 2023 a video on the Chinese social media platform BiliBili showed what was claimed to be LK-99 levitating. Levitation is a sign of the Meisner effect where it expels all magnetic fields causing levitation above magnets. This effect is a property of all superconducting materials but is not unique to superconductors. The levitation of LK-99 would change public sentiment from skepticism to faith as it felt more real than ever. However, it was not all sunshine and rainbows as buried within the maelstrom of posts on the levitation were the troubling posts of LK-99 being a facade of superconductivity. The hype though would prevent these revelations from gaining traction for quite some time.

The floating rock was enough for the public to be convinced and I don't blame them because the effect was straight out of science fiction and it didn't help the discourse promoted the idea that it was a silver bullet. After the first week of August not a single lab showed an abrupt drop to 0 electrical resistance. Electrical Resistance is generally just how well an object can conduct electricity and a sudden drop to 0 resistance is what defines a superconductor. The hope of the public waned as no conclusive evidence was published. The final nail in LK-99's coffin came on August 16th, 2023 in an article by Nature that showed it was not a superconductor. LK-99 was an insulator or a material that actively stops electricity from being conducted through it. The reason that it was thought to be a superconductor was because it contained impurities from the creation process causing the superconductive effects and the inconsistent results from different labs. It was fool's gold for material scientists that resolved quite anticlimactically.

So it was all a misunderstanding from a paper that hadn't even gone through peer review right? Science for the public is about the big discoveries and this discovery was simply a dud but there are far more beneath the surface left to uncover. The true story of LK-99 lies not in the material but the people behind it. While it cannot be proven to be malicious, from the very beginning the authors' actions and the data presented were suspect. Questions arise like why would a scientist fake findings or claim a lie? What is the point of risking your reputation by uploading it to Arxiv? What is the proper process? The partial answer lies in the system in which papers are selected and published and it all starts with the question of who uploaded to Arxiv anyway.

Misconduct or Mistake?

The two major scientists for LK-99 are Sukbae Lee and Jihoon Kim whose initials are where the material's name is derived from. However, these two people, while both being the first authors or the primary people behind the paper, were not the ones who uploaded the paper. The situation is strange as there were two papers uploaded to Arxiv about LK-99 but the papers were completely different. The first paper that caused the initial hype was uploaded by Young-Wan Kwon while the second paper uploaded after the first by Hyuntak Kim doesn't even include Kwon as an author.

It is completely normal to edit papers before being published but having two completely different papers from one another that don't even have the same authors is extremely unusual. Hyuntak Kim even said the first paper by Kwon was uploaded without his permission and had many defects. Kwon's true motivations may never be known but from the outside, it feels that Kwon was trying to steal a bit of the acknowledgement by having his name on it compared to the other paper. No matter the reason this is not proper conduct whatsoever and casts doubt on the entire discovery if the researchers can't even get the authors straight. Aside from the upload oddity, the graphs in the second version uploaded by Hyuntak Kim also have defects.

Graph From Second Paper

According to Professor Philip Moriarty from the YouTube channel Sixty Symbols the presentation of data is almost laughable. In the graph above the units on the y-axis are too big to show 0 resistivity of LK-99. He pointed out how even a "first year physics textbook" would tell you the units should have been 10^-8 cm, not 10^-2 cm. Furthermore, a superconductor is supposed to have a sudden drop to a resistance of 0 that the graph doesn't actually show and instead shows a slow eventual drop to what seems to be 0 indicated by the black squiggly line near the x-axis. The odd drop-off is already strange but the wrong units are just unacceptable for a paper done by researchers.

The authors promised to fix the mistakes and claimed better results that proved without a doubt it is a room temperature superconductor but it's 2025 and their promise has still not materialized. The consensus on this debacle is that they should've just had the paper published and gone through peer review. The answer is always apparently just peer review but why is it so important and would it have prevented LK-99 from happening?

What's the Deal with Peer Review?

The common solution that every scientist comes up with when it comes to cases like LK-99 is to just not care about papers until it has been properly peer reviewed and published in some scientific journal. Peer review is always thrown around as the answer and is considered fundamental to the scientific system but surprisingly this process is not as clearly defined as one would think. Generally speaking peer review is where a paper is thoroughly checked and vetted by another scientist working in the same field. The scientist would then say if the paper is indeed good or if it needs edits. Simple right?

This definition is on the surface reasonable but there is no clear ruleset for peer review. The matter is instead decided by the journal that would publish the paper. In Nature a well known scientific journal, the editors will first pick the papers that seem to be most intriguing, and then once selected the editor will choose scientists to peer review the paper. There are usually 2-3 reviewers and all of them are volunteers not paid by Nature. The editor gets to make all the decisions regarding who to send it to and the reviewers stay anonymous unless they willingly reveal themselves. After receiving feedback the editor will make a final decision on whether to publish or not. It's clear that for a process so core to the scientific system it feels oddly unscientific and arbitrary.

Diagram by Me

According to a study on peer review by Jonathan P. Tennant and Tony Ross-Hellauer there is very little scientific research done on peer review. The paper concludes that many aspects of peer review don't have enough scientific data to support or undermine them. Worryingly, the paper also points out how peer review often feels like a black box where what exactly happens during peer review is unclear. There are no rulesets for everyone to follow and journals are free to change practically anything they want. The situation worsens as the job of peer review is extremely undervalued and scientists that do the reviews may not have the baseline skills necessary to conduct a proper review.

In fact, LK-99 may be a demonstration of the issues with the inconsistency of peer review. According to Sukbae Lee he did try to publish their findings in Nature but was required to publish in another specialized journal. Lee then subsequently had it published in a domestic academic journal in April before the initial uploads to Arxiv. This means that LK-99 did have peer review but it wasn't able to prevent the event from occurring. While I am speculating, the shoddy peer review may have given Kwon the confidence to upload that first paper on Arxiv.

How Do We Improve?

I will say peer review is not fundamentally flawed and prestigious journals like Nature do make it work as the papers published are usually high quality. But that does not mean the process doesn't need reforms. In fact, the disparity in quality of peer review between journals is a major reason for why it needs change.

There is this sentiment that I always hear where researchers should only publish to the most prestigious journals because that is the only way to really get taken seriously by other researchers or organizations. This sucks because it's just impossible for everyone to get published in these prestigious journals even if the quality of the research may be the same. I feel that if peer review was disconnected from journals where every journal follows the same core peer review process then I think this sentiment would be far less pervasive and give researchers far more avenues of success.

The peer review study believes that there needs to be more rigorous research done on peer review. I agree that peer review should not be put on a pedestal and needs to be an established field of research just like other topics in science. By having good hard research on peer review the black box nature of the process may be lifted.

These are only some possible changes and other suggestions may be even more effective but my point is that there are significant areas for improvement in the current established system that should not be ignored. Science is an iterative process and is never finished and that should include the way we conduct science itself.
